The Great Divorce
C. S. Lewis

Date Finished: August 2022


The Great Divorce is similar to Till We Have Faces in some ways. They both are works of fiction that tie into Lewis's theology and morality background, and both have interesting themes with metaphorical beats. But I would The Great Divorce is much more closely related to Lewis's non-fiction than Till We Have Faces. There is a good preface where he mentions that this work is by no means supposed to be a theological work on what Heaven and Hell are like. No one knows and no one will know until we die what those are like. Lewis also includes the very last chapter probably for the same reason: keeping it a dream diffuses speculation that this is what Lewis's idea of Heaven is. And I find that favorable, because then we can focus on the morality of an eternal life with God, and not deal with a highly speculative account of Heaven. Lewis creates a story where he goes along a path into Heaven from Hell, where he views stories and situations from an outside point of view. Many interesting arguments brought up, my favorite by far being the Scottish man, whom he calls teacher, explain the selfishness and self-choice of Hell compared to the reality and joyfulness of Heaven (Chapter 9). I thought the ending, like Till We Have Faces, could have had a more clear and satisfying situation. The whole pitying vice was a bit confusing, but had a nice tie-in with Jesus at the end. This book teeters the line between morality and fiction, which I really enjoyed. I found a lot of points useful from this book, similar to The Screwtape Letters.
